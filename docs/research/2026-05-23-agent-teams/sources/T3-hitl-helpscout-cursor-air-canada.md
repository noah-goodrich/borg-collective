## Key Findings

1. **Air Canada (Feb 2024):** Bereavement-fare chatbot invented a refund policy; customer
   Jake Moffatt sued and won. Air Canada's defense — that "the chatbot was a separate legal
   entity" — was rejected. Companies are liable for what their AI says.
2. **Cursor (April 2025):** Support bot named "Sam" fabricated a no-multi-device login policy
   in a customer email. Spread on Hacker News, triggered subscription cancellations. Cursor
   ended up labeling AI responses explicitly.
3. **"First filter" is misleading framing.** For customers, the AI's response is often the
   ENTIRE interaction with the company, not a filter behind which a human exists.
4. **Generative AI cannot be trustworthy in the way a person can.** "They can't differentiate
   reality from hallucination because everything they produce is, from the perspective of
   generative AI, a hallucination."
5. **Trust loss is hard to measure but durable.** "What's much harder to measure is the loss
   of trust when an AI bot gives out a confidently incorrect answer." Damage compounds with
   each silent failure.
