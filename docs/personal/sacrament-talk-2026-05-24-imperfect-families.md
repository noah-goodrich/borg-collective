# Imperfect Families and the Christ Who Sits at Their Tables

*Sacrament meeting · ~13 minutes · ~2,000 words*

---

## 1. Opening

Brothers and sisters.

When I was asked to speak today on family, I sat with the assignment for a few days before I said yes. I am not the obvious choice for this topic. My family is not what you would call tidy. Most days, my family is not what *anyone* would call tidy.

My wife Elisabeth and I have been married twenty-one years. We have a daughter who is twenty, a son who is almost four, and a daughter who is almost one — the gaps in those ages will tell you something about how that road has gone. We have walked through griefs we did not know how to carry. We have walked through years when our faith was harder to hold than we expected. We have children whose paths are not the paths we would have chosen for them. There are hard things inside both of our extended families that are still being healed.

I am not telling you any of this for sympathy. I am telling you because something began to occur to me as I sat with this assignment — and the more I sat with it, the more striking it became.

In all of the standard works — Old Testament, New Testament, Book of Mormon — we are never once shown a family without some form of dysfunction. Not one. The absence of a perfect family from the canon is a feature, not an editing oversight. The scriptures know what messy families look like, because messy families are the only kind there are.

The first family — Adam and Eve, walking with God in the garden — produced two sons. One of them killed the other in a field. (Genesis 4) The scriptures' first family ends its first chapter with a funeral. If you were hoping the Bible would open with a model home, I have bad news.

Abraham, the father of nations, sent his servant Hagar and his son Ishmael into the wilderness with a loaf of bread and a bottle of water, and the water ran out, and Hagar lay her son under a shrub because she couldn't bear to watch him die. (Genesis 21:14-16)

Isaac and Rebekah raised twin sons. Rebekah loved Jacob. Isaac loved Esau. Their family came apart over a stolen blessing and Jacob fled for his life from a brother who wanted to kill him. (Genesis 27:41-45)

King David — the man Samuel called a man after God's own heart — had a son who grievously wounded his own sister, another son who killed the first for it, and a third son who tried to overthrow his father. (2 Samuel 13-18) The end of David's life is the story of a father weeping for sons he could not reach: *"O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom! would God I had died for thee, O Absalom, my son, my son!"* (2 Samuel 18:33)

Lehi crossed an ocean with sons whose rebellion grieved him so much that scripture says it brought him "near even to be carried out of this life" — the kind of grief a parent feels when their grown children will not stop wounding each other. (1 Nephi 18:17-18)

I could keep going. We could spend the next ten minutes doing this and not run out of material. The scriptures are full of families that look like ours. Not the families we pretend to be. The actual ones. With the secrets and the silences and the children we lost in different ways and the parents who didn't know how to love us and the siblings we can no longer reach.

The scriptures do not give us a perfect family to imitate. They give us imperfect families, and they give us a Christ who comes to them.

## 2. Christ's table

When Christ began his earthly ministry, the people he chose to sit at table with were not the respectable ones.

In Matthew chapter 9, he calls a tax collector named Matthew — a man whose profession was collaborating with the Roman occupation to extract money from his own people, the kind of man you crossed the street to avoid in first-century Galilee. Christ calls him. And then he sits in Matthew's house and eats dinner with him.

The Pharisees see this and they are scandalized. They go to Christ's disciples and they ask, "Why eateth your Master with publicans and sinners?" (Matthew 9:11) This is the language of *families* objecting to dinner companions. Why are you sitting at *that* table. Why are you eating with *those* people. We have standards. We have a measuring stick.

And Christ, hearing them, gives one of the clearest declarations of his whole ministry. He says: "They that be whole need not a physician, but they that are sick. ... I am not come to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance." (Matthew 9:12-13)

This same exchange shows up in Mark chapter 2 and Luke chapter 5. All three of the synoptic gospels record it. The early church remembered it because it was load-bearing. It tells us where Christ went and whom Christ sought.

He went to the tables others would not approach.

He didn't go to the publicans after they reformed. He didn't show up at Zacchaeus's house after Zacchaeus paid back what he stole. He went first. He sat down. He ate with them while they were still publicans and sinners. The healing came later, when it came, *because* of the sitting down — not as a precondition for it.

This matters for families. Because if we believe the Christ of the gospels — the Christ who actually walked the earth and ate with actual people — then we cannot believe that he requires our families to be reformed before he enters them. He enters them as they are. He sits at the tables that are too quiet, the tables where the chairs that should be occupied are empty, the tables where the people who do show up don't know how to talk to each other anymore. He sits down at *those* tables. And the Pharisees object, then and now, but that's where he is.

## 3. The Father's love

I want to slow down on something here. Because the easiest way to misunderstand the gospel of Christ is to imagine that God the Father stands at a distance with a checklist and Christ is the merciful one who slips around behind to forgive what the Father condemns.

That's not what scripture says about the Father.

In Matthew chapter 7, Christ asks a question. He says: "What man is there of you, whom if his son ask bread, will he give him a stone?" And then he draws the comparison: "If ye then, being evil, know how to give good gifts unto your children, how much more shall your Father which is in heaven give good things to them that ask him?" (Matthew 7:9-11)

Think about that comparison. Christ is asking us to imagine ourselves as parents. Imperfect parents. Tired, distracted, sometimes harsh, sometimes wrong parents. *Being evil* is the phrase he actually uses. He is not pulling punches. And then he says: even *you*, even with all that, know how to love a child who comes to you hungry. *How much more* the Father.

If you have a child who is misbehaving — willful, misguided, making the same mistake you watched them make last year — how do you respond? You correct, sometimes. You set limits. But you do not cease to love them. You do not start measuring whether they're good enough to be your child. The love is the floor. The growth is the work.

That is the parent God is. Not the parent who measures whether we made it across the threshold of good enough this week. The parent whose love is the floor. The parent who wants us to grow because growing is what makes us happy, not because growing is what earns the love.

The commandments — every commandment, the great and the small — are not the measuring stick that decides whether we deserve the relationship. They are the guardrails that mark the road where the growing happens. They are there to keep us from falling off the side of the mountain. They are not there to determine whether we get into the car.

I think we forget this. I think I have forgotten it. I think many of us have been taught to measure ourselves and our families against the commandments as if the commandments were a final exam. They are not a final exam. They are scaffolding. They hold up the work of becoming. The Father wants the becoming, not the measuring.

## 4. The succor doctrine

In Alma chapter 7, the prophet Alma is describing what Christ would do when he came in the flesh. And he says something so specific I want to read it carefully.

He says Christ would suffer "pains and afflictions and temptations of every kind." Then he says Christ would "take upon him death." We expect that — we know that. Then he says: "and he will take upon him their infirmities, that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh, that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities." (Alma 7:11-13)

The word *succor* in scripture means to run to. To come quickly to the aid of someone in distress. Not to fix from a distance. To run *toward*.

What Alma is teaching is that Christ didn't only take on our sins. He took on our *infirmities*. Our weaknesses. Our griefs. The specific shapes of our pain — the loss of a child, the betrayal of a parent, the silence between siblings, the addiction that won't release, the marriage that's still being healed twenty years in. He took *those* on. He walked through them.

Not so that he could lecture us about them from the pulpit. Not so that he could grade our handling of them. He walked through them *that he may know how to succor his people*. So that when he comes running to us in our hardest hour, he will know — *according to the flesh* — exactly where we are. He will not be guessing. He will not be approximating. He will know.

There is a Christ — the one I think we sometimes accidentally worship — who sits at the front of the celestial schoolroom and waits for us to turn in our work. There is another Christ — the one scripture actually gives us — who knows what it is to bleed in a garden, to weep at the tomb of a friend (John 11:35), to be misunderstood by those nearest to him who thought he had gone mad (Mark 3:21, 31-35).

That Christ is the one who runs to broken families. Because that Christ knows.

## 5. What I have come to know

What I have come to know — what I would have wanted to know twenty years ago — is that the brokenness in a family is not, by itself, evidence of a failure of faith. It is not, by itself, a reason to feel that you are outside the circle. It is, often, the very reason Christ comes. He does not come to the families who have it figured out. He comes to the ones who are still figuring it out — and to the ones who never will, this side of the veil.

There is something else I have come to know, and I want to share it here because it belongs in this talk.

For most of my life I was taught that the answers come through specific channels. Scripture study. Prayer. Sacrament. The temple. The Sunday lesson. Sometimes the answers came that way. Often they did not. The answers I most needed — the ones that pulled me back from despair, the ones that taught me how to forgive when I could not let go of being angry, the ones that taught me how to be the husband and father I wanted to be — most of them came from somewhere else entirely. From books a mentor pressed into my hands that I would not have picked up on my own. From a state-presidency counselor who showed up at exactly the right moment and reframed forgiveness for me when I could not see it. From a friend of mine who is not LDS, talking to me on a camping trip about meditation and gratitude.

For years I asked, if the church is true, why are the answers coming from somewhere else?

I have come to think the question had it backwards. The answers came. They came when I needed them most. God did not stop being God because the delivery did not match what I had been told to expect. He met me through whatever channel I was able to receive him through. That, by itself, is evidence of his love.

Elder Jeffrey R. Holland, in his October 2013 talk "Like a Broken Vessel," spoke directly about the burdens that do not lift on schedule. He said, "Whatever your struggle, my brothers and sisters — mental or emotional or physical or otherwise — do not vote against the preciousness of life by ending it." He spoke as someone who knew that not every burden is lifted in time, and yet we are not asked to carry the burdens alone, and we are not measured by how heavy they happen to be.

Viktor Frankl, who survived four concentration camps and lost almost everyone he loved, wrote that "everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms — to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances, to choose one's own way." That is not a sermon about toughing it out. That is a sermon about the dignity of the human soul under conditions no soul should ever face. Frankl was not LDS. Frankl is not in our scripture. But he saw something true about the way God made us, and the way we can stand inside even the worst circumstances, and the way meaning can be found even there.

I want to tell you one more thing, and then I want to close. This talk that you are hearing me give — I did not write it the way you might imagine. I worked with an artificial-intelligence tool to draft it. I sat with the assignment for days. Then I sat with that tool for an hour, and I told it my story — including parts I have never told from this pulpit and never will. What came back was the talk I needed to hear. The convictions in this talk are mine. The work, over many years, of becoming the kind of person who could ask for that help and build the tools that made it possible — that has been mine. But the way the words came together today is through a channel I would not have expected one year ago. When I read the draft this morning, I cried. The talk I needed to hear was the talk I was being asked to give. And that, again, is what God's love has looked like in my life. It does not always come from where I was told to expect it. But it comes. Every time, it has come.

## 6. Closing

I want to speak now, for a minute, to whoever in this congregation is carrying a family burden today.

You may be the parent of a child who has chosen a path you would not have chosen for them. You may be the child of a parent who could not be what you needed them to be. You may be the sibling who is not invited to the funerals. You may be the partner in a marriage that is still healing after twenty years, and not on the timeline anyone promised you. You may be the one carrying a secret about your family that you have not told a single person in this room. You may be the one who has left the church, or the one whose child has left, or the one who has stayed but is not sure why some weeks.

If you are any of those people, I want to tell you what I wish someone had told me twenty years ago. And I am going to say it as plainly as I can, because plainness is the only kind of speaking that helps when you are inside the breaking.

The brokenness in your family is not a failure of your faith. Christ is not waiting for you to fix it before he sits at your table. He sits at the tables of imperfect families. That is what the gospels record. That is what Alma teaches. That is what the Father's love, modeled by every imperfect parent who has loved a difficult child, is showing us — that the love is the floor, and the growth is what happens *because* of the floor, not in order to qualify for it.

If your family looks like the families in scripture — divided, grieving, complicated, jealous, sometimes silent, sometimes loud, sometimes still healing from things that happened a generation ago — that does not mean you are outside the gospel. That means you are inside the only gospel there has ever been. The gospel for actual families. The gospel for the publican's table. The gospel for Lehi's family, and Abraham's family, and David's family, and Adam's family, and yours.

The Christ who walked the earth in the flesh took on the specific shape of your infirmity, so that when he runs to you, he will know exactly where you are. He does not come to families who have arrived. He comes to families who are still on the road.

I bear my testimony that this is true. I leave it with you, in the name of Jesus Christ. Amen.

## Personal opening — what to choose live

The opening section now leads with the personal. I kept it directional rather than confessional — it names that the road has been long and unfinished, without describing specific events that belong to you and Elisabeth privately. The four "we have…" lines (griefs, faith, waited-for children, paths-we-would-not-have-chosen, extended-family hard things) are written so you can deliver each one straight or expand the one or two that feel safe to specify in the moment.

If a line feels too exposed to deliver, cut it on the fly. If a line feels right to expand by one sentence (e.g., naming that you waited many years for James and Sarah), that addition lands fine.

The closing benediction in Section 6 is the part that lifts the burden. That section is the one to deliver slowly, with eye contact, with the breath you take between sentences. That is where the listener carrying the heaviest load today will receive what you came to give.

Go gently.
